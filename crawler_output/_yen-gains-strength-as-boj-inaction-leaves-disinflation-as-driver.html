Yen Under Pressure to Extend World-Beating Rally Against Dollar

Kevin Buckland
Shigeki Nozawa
May 1, 2016 — 11:00 AM EDT
Share on FacebookShare on TwitterShare on WhatsApp
Share on LinkedInShare on RedditShare on Google+E-mailShare on TwitterShare on WhatsApp
  • `Currency market is in a rather dangerous zone': Credit Suisse
  • Swiss bank sees potential for yen to reach 90 per dollar
Share on FacebookShare on TwitterShare on WhatsApp
Share on LinkedInShare on RedditShare on Google+E-mailShare on TwitterShare on WhatsApp

The yen’s world-beating rally against the dollar looks to be gathering momentum, as central bank inaction on both sides of the Pacific Ocean leaves inflation expectations to drive the exchange rate.

Japan’s currency surged to an 18-month high on Friday after Bank of Japan Governor Haruhiko Kuroda refrained from adding to stimulus the day before. That took its gain this year to 13 percent, the most among its developed-market peers. The BOJ’s decision came just hours after Federal Reserve Chair Janet Yellen frustrated dollar bulls by reiterating she’s in no rush to cool the economy by raising interest rates.

With consumer price pressures building in the U.S. and dissipating in Japan, the effect is a narrowing gap in so-called real yields -- or the return that an investor can expect after accounting for inflation -- which is supporting yen strength. If both central banks stay on the sidelines, Credit Suisse Group AG projects Japan’s currency could rapidly appreciate toward 90 per dollar.

“So long as the Fed signals that they are being cautious in raising rates, real yields in the U.S. will decline, leading the dollar weaker,” said Hiromichi Shirakawa, the Swiss lender’s chief Japan economist and a former BOJ official. “The currency market is in a rather dangerous zone.”

Inflation Expectations

The BOJ’s benchmark for measuring progress toward its 2 percent target showed prices retreated at an annual 0.3 percent pace in March, the biggest decline since April 2013, the month that Kuroda initiated his stimulus program. It had previously hovered near zero for more than a year.

By contrast, the Fed’s preferred measure of inflation, based on the prices of goods and services consumers buy, rose 0.8 percent in the year through March. The so-called core measure, which strips out food and energy prices, climbed 1.6 percent.

That’s seen a Treasury market gauge of inflation expectations over the coming decade -- called the break-even rate -- jump to 1.7 percent from as low as 1.2 percent in February. The equivalent measure in Japan is languishing at 0.3 percent.

Adjusting benchmark 10-year Treasury yields for the break-even rate gives an expected real yield of around 0.1 percent, compared with about minus 0.4 percent in Japan.

The yen has kicked off to its best start to a year in more than two decades amid increased demand for haven assets in an environment of uncertain global growth. The currency strengthened to as high as 106.28 per dollar on Friday, the strongest since October 2014. Japanese markets were closed on Friday for a national holiday.

“Based on the global economic condition and investors’ risk sentiment, the major trend in the fundamentals is for a stronger yen,” said Shinichiro Kadota, a Tokyo-based foreign-exchange strategist at Barclays Plc. “We see the fair value at around 95 to 100 and it will head toward that level.”
