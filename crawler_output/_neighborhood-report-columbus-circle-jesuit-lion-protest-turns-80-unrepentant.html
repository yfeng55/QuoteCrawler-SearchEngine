NEIGHBORHOOD REPORT: COLUMBUS CIRCLE; A Jesuit Lion Of Protest Turns 80, Unrepentant

By KATHERINE MARSH
Published: May 13, 2001

The braids and beards were a little grayer, peace stories had become old war stories, and there were grandchildren now, ogling the sun and moon puppets. Still, 800 friends and admirers of the Jesuit activist Daniel Berrigan gathered last Sunday at St. Paul the Apostle Church, on Ninth Avenue and 60th Street, to mark his 80th birthday.

The granddaddy of the Catholic protest movement, Father Berrigan sat in the front of the hall, wearing a blue shirt with a bald eagle embroidered on the back. Surrounded by family and old friends (though missing his brother and partner-in-protest, Philip, who was in prison in Ohio for hammering on a bomber), he looked particularly prophetlike, even if his birthday party evoked the past more than the future.

On a stage decorated with origami cranes, the folk singer Dar Williams warbled ''I Had No Right,'' a song she wrote about Father Berrigan and the Catonsville Nine, who famously burned Vietnam draft files in 1968. Friends sat on folding chairs, munching Ben & Jerry's popsicles under banners commemorating old liberation theologists and denouncing the bombing of Hiroshima.

Michael Short, a clean-cut 24-year-old high school teacher from Scranton, Pa., who teaches a class called Topics in Justice, first met Mr. Berrigan two years ago through Jesuit volunteer activities.

''I asked him how things were going,'' Mr. Short recalled fondly, ''And he said: 'Well, things are a little weird. I haven't been in jail for a while.' ''

Things have gotten back to normal for Father Berrigan, who was arrested just last month, on Good Friday, for blocking the entrance to the Intrepid Sea-Air-Space Museum, which, he says, honors instruments of death. One of those arrested with him was John Dear, a fellow member of the West Side Jesuit Community, who was M.C. of the party.

''In many ways, Dan has been forgotten in the U.S.,'' Father Dear, his protégé, said with a sigh. ''But he continues to inspire us as people of nonviolence.''

At the evening's end, Father Berrigan stood up to receive a long line of well-wishers. ''I feel like it's my wake,'' he said drily, clutching a bouquet of irises. But he was very alive, and to the delight of his admirers, he predicted many future arrests.

''The day after I'm embalmed,'' he declared, ''that's when I'll give it up.'' KATHERINE MARSH
